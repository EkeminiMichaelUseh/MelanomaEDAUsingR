Exploratory Data Analysis of
"Survival from malignant melanoma" Dataset (By Ekemini Useh)

Introduction

One type of skin cancer that is mostly brought on by UV rays from the sun is called melanoma. Because human skin is exposed to this dangerous beam daily, further research is important. This report's purposes are to:
- Ascertain the data's summary statistics.
- Analyze the variables using correlation and regression.
- To ascertain whether a patient's sex has an impact on survival time after surgery by a two-sample significance test grouped by patient sex.
- The percentage of malignant melanoma patients who survive after surgery to remove their tumors.

Please click here to see the full documentation on github.

About Data

Data Source / Description

The dataset is the measurements recorded on 205 patients with malignant melanoma whose tumors were removed at the Department of Plastic Surgery, University Hospital of Odense, Denmark, between 1962 and 1977. The data collected were survival time, patient’s status, sex, age, year of surgery, thickness of tumor, and ulcer (if tumor was ulcerated).

Data Cleaning and Preparation

The tool used for data cleaning and preparation is R

Steps Taken
- The less important column was removed.
- The data types of variables status, sex, year, and ulcer were changed from double to factor.
- The values in status, sex, and ulcer were represented in numbers, the labels were changed to the actual inputs for easy data exploration.
- Null values were checked but there was none.

Correlation and Regression Analysis,
Two-sample T-test grouped by sex

- Correlation and Regression

Time ~ Thickness

Using Pearson’s correlation, the relationship has a weak negative correlation of -0.2354087. This means as the tumor thickness gets bigger, the smaller the survival time a patient lives after the procedure.

The line of best fit is: time = 2413.41 – 89.25 thickness here, the y-intercept is 2413.41 when the thickness is equal to zero. The gradient is -89.25. For every unit increase in the value of thickness, time is predicted to decrease by 89.25, on average. The residual standard error is 1093 and the R-squared is 0.05542. Due to the unreliability of this model, using it for any predictive analysis of the relationship should be done with caution.

Time ~ Age

The relationship has a weak negative correlation of -0.3015179. This means that as age increases, a patient lives fewer days after the operation.

The line of best fit is: time = 3217.45 – 20.29 age here, the y-intercept is 3217.45 when the age is equal to zero. The gradient is -20.29. For every unit increase in the value of age, time is predicted to decrease by 20.29, on average. The residual standard error is 1072 and the R-squared is 0.09091. Due to the unreliability of this model, using it for any predictive analysis of the relationship should be done with caution.

Thickness ~ Age

The relationship is a weak positive correlation of 0.2124798. This means as the age increases, the bigger the tumor thickness.

The line of best fit is: thickness = 0.94105 + 0.03772 age here, the y-intercept is 0.94105 when the age is equal to zero. The gradient is 0.03772. For every unit increase in the value of age value, thickness is predicted to increase by 0.03772, on average. The residual standard error is 2.899 and the R-squared is 0.04515. Due to the unreliability of this model, using it for any predictive analysis of the relationship should be done with caution.

- Two-sample t-test

Using t-test Where Ho = Null hypothesis, H1 = alternative hypothesis The default level of significance α = 0.05

Time grouped by sex

Ho: The mean survival time after the operation is the same for both sexes


H1: The mean survival time after the operation is different in both sexes.

The p-value = 0.03868 which is smaller than α = 0.05. Therefore, we can reject Ho and conclude that there is evidence that the true mean survival time after the operation is different depending on whether the patient is male or female.

Thickness grouped by sex

Ho: The mean thickness of the tumor is the same for both sexes

H1: The mean thickness of the tumor is different in both sexes.

The p-value = 0.01009 which is smaller than α = 0.05. Therefore, we can reject Ho and conclude that there is evidence that the true mean thickness of the tumor is different depending on whether the patient is male or female.

Age grouped by sex

Ho: The mean age of the patients is the same in both sexes

H1: The mean age of the patients is different depending in both sexes.

The p-value = 0.3408 which is greater than α = 0.05. Therefore, we cannot reject Ho. We can conclude that there is evidence that the true mean age of patients is the same in male and female.

Observations

- The findings gathered from the data show that malignant melanoma is likely to have ulcerations present depending on its tumor thickness.

- Less people are probably going to die from their melanoma because more people live after having their cancer removed.

- When melanoma tumors ulcerate because of their increased thickness, the chance of death from the disease raises.

Recommendations, Conclusion, and Limitations

Recommendations

People need to seek proper medical care and advice at the sight of any skin infection as it could be melanoma. This will hinder the growth and spread of such infection. The possibility of ulceration, if detected early, will decrease and cured without the risk of fatality.

Conclusion

- Melanoma is not fata when detected early.

- The relationship between survival time and tumor thickness is a weak negative correlation. The smaller the tumor thickness, the higher the survival time afetr surgery.

- Time and age had a weak negative correlation. The patient's survival time following melanoma removal decreases with age.

- A weak positive association exists between age and thickness. A larger tumor was possible in the elderly people.
